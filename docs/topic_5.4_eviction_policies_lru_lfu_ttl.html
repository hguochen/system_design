5.4 Eviction Policies — LRU, LFU, TTL

00 🗺️ Topic Overview

What This Topic Is About

Cache memory is finite. When a cache fills up, it must decide which entries to remove to make room for new ones — this decision is governed by an eviction policy. The policy you choose directly affects cache hit rate, latency, and correctness. The central tension is memory utilization vs. hit rate vs. data freshness: no single policy wins in all workloads, and a wrong choice can mean your cache underperforms a naively larger one.

🎯 What to Focus On

  • 1. What each policy optimizes forLRU optimizes for recency, LFU optimizes for frequency, TTL optimizes for freshness. Each assumes a different access pattern. Matching the policy to the workload is the key skill.
  • 2. Data structures behind LRU and LFULRU uses a doubly linked list + hashmap for O(1) get/put. LFU uses a frequency-bucketed doubly linked list structure for O(1). These come up in coding interviews too — know them cold.
  • 3. When LFU beats LRUIn Zipfian (frequency-skewed) workloads — top songs, viral posts, CDN objects — LFU dramatically outperforms LRU. Candidates who only know LRU get exposed here.
  • 4. TTL is not the same kind of policyTTL is a freshness/consistency mechanism, not a memory-pressure algorithm. Production systems combine TTL (for data validity) with LRU or LFU (for memory management) — they serve different purposes.
  • 5. Cache pollution — LRU's silent killerA sequential scan evicts all hot data from an LRU cache. Know the failure mode and the mitigations: LFU, 2Q, or LRU-K. This is a common deep-dive probe in interviews.

05 🧠 Core Definition

What is it, in one sentence?

An eviction policy is the algorithm a cache uses to decide which entry to remove when it reaches capacity. The three dominant policies are LRU (evict the least recently accessed entry), LFU (evict the least frequently accessed entry), and TTL (evict entries whose age exceeds a configured threshold — a freshness mechanism rather than a pure memory-pressure algorithm).

06 📦 Core Concepts

The essential building blocks — terms and ideas you must have solid before going deeper.

LRU — Least Recently Used

LRU maintains an implicit temporal ordering: the entry accessed furthest back in time is the first eviction candidate. It assumes temporal locality — recently used data is likely to be used again soon. Implemented in O(1) for both get and put using a doubly linked list (to maintain access order) combined with a hashmap (for O(1) key lookup). On every access, the node moves to the head; eviction removes the tail.

LFU — Least Frequently Used

LFU tracks access count per entry and evicts the entry with the lowest count, assuming frequency locality — popular items stay popular. LFU outperforms LRU in Zipfian (frequency-skewed) workloads, e.g., top songs, viral posts, CDN content. The naive implementation uses a min-heap (O(log n)), but an O(1) implementation exists using a doubly linked list of frequency buckets plus a min-frequency pointer.

TTL — Time To Live

TTL is a time-based expiry mechanism: each entry is stamped with a creation time and invalidated once elapsed time exceeds the configured TTL. TTL is primarily about data freshness and consistency — it prevents stale data from being served even if the cache isn't full. Most systems combine TTL with LRU or LFU: TTL answers "is this data still valid?", while LRU/LFU answers "which entry to remove when full?"

Cache Pollution

Cache pollution occurs when a workload (sequential scan, batch job) reads a large volume of one-time-use data, evicting hot, frequently reused entries from an LRU cache. LRU has no knowledge of access frequency, so it faithfully evicts the "least recently used" entries — which happen to be your hot dataset. Solutions: LFU (immune to scans — cold scan entries never accrue frequency), 2Q (probationary queue for new entries, protected LRU queue for those accessed twice), or LRU-K (evict based on K-th most recent access).

Approximate vs. Exact Eviction

Exact LRU and LFU require maintaining precise access metadata, which has memory and CPU overhead. Production systems use approximate implementations: Redis LRU randomly samples a configurable number of keys (default 5) and evicts the least recent among the sample. Redis LFU uses a logarithmic counter (0–255) with a configurable decay factor to approximate historical frequency without unbounded counter growth. These approximations are fast and sufficiently accurate at scale.

07 🔍 First Principles — Why Does This Exist?

What fundamental problem does this concept solve? Why was it invented?

Cache memory is finite — typically orders of magnitude smaller than the dataset it serves. As new entries arrive, old ones must be discarded. Without a policy, you're forced to either reject new entries (no-eviction, which throws OOM errors at capacity) or remove entries randomly (random eviction, which has unpredictable hit rates and can evict the hottest entry in the dataset). Both are terrible at scale.

The core insight is that not all cached entries are equally valuable. Entries recently accessed are likely to be accessed again (temporal locality). Entries accessed many times are likely to remain popular (frequency locality). Entries older than a known validity window are likely wrong or stale. Eviction policies formalize these intuitions into deterministic algorithms that maximize hit rate for a given cache size.

The stakes are high: a cache with an 80% hit rate vs. a 95% hit rate can mean the difference between 5× and 20× reduction in database load — from the same hardware budget. Poor eviction policy is often the silent cause of unexpectedly high cache miss rates in production systems that look correctly configured on the surface.

08 🗺️ Mental Models

Intuition frames that help you reason about this concept fast — especially under interview pressure.

Model 1: The Bookshelf

Imagine a physical bookshelf with limited space. LRU means: when you need to add a new book, remove the one you picked up least recently. LFU means: remove the one you've opened the fewest times total. TTL means: any book older than 6 months gets removed regardless of access. The failure mode of LRU is obvious here — if you do one big research project reading 50 rarely-used books sequentially, all your favorite frequently-read books get pushed off the shelf. LFU would protect them because they have high access counts.

Where this breaks down: The bookshelf model doesn't capture the counter-decay issue in LFU — in real systems, a book that was very popular 2 years ago but hasn't been touched since will have an artificially high count and won't be evicted. Redis LFU's decay mechanism is the fix.

Model 2: The Frequency Leaderboard

Think of LFU as a leaderboard ranked by access count. New entries start at count=1, at the bottom. Every access moves an entry up one rung. Eviction always removes from the bottom rung. The insight: a scan workload reads 10,000 keys once each — they all land at count=1 and get evicted first, never disturbing high-frequency entries. The weakness: entries that were popular a long time ago but are now cold maintain high counts and resist eviction. The decay factor in Redis LFU gradually reduces counts for stale entries, allowing formerly-popular but now-cold entries to become eviction candidates.

Model 3: TTL as a Freshness Contract

TTL is best understood as a contract between the cache and the data source: "I guarantee that this cached value is no more than X seconds out of date." This makes it fundamentally different from LRU/LFU — those optimize memory utilization; TTL optimizes data correctness. The practical failure modes are: stale reads when TTL is too long, and high miss rates plus cache stampedes when TTL is too short. The right TTL is tied to how fast the underlying data changes, not to memory pressure. Combining TTL with LRU gives you both freshness and memory efficiency — they're orthogonal concerns.

09 ⚙️ How It Works — Mechanics

Step-by-step explanation of the internal mechanism.

LRU — O(1) Implementation

Two data structures: a doubly linked list (head = most recently used, tail = least recently used) and a hashmap mapping keys to their list nodes.

GET(key): Look up the node via hashmap. If found (hit), move the node to the head of the list. Return value. O(1). PUT(key, value): If key exists, update value and move to head. If not: create node, insert at head. If at capacity, remove the tail node and delete from hashmap. O(1). Eviction target: always the tail node.

LFU — O(1) Implementation

Three structures: a key→value map, a key→frequency map, and a frequency→[ordered keys] doubly linked list map. Also track minFrequency.

GET(key): Look up value. Increment key's frequency. Move key from frequency bucket F to F+1. If bucket F is now empty and F == minFrequency, increment minFrequency. O(1). PUT(key, value): If at capacity, evict the LRU entry from the minFrequency bucket (each frequency bucket is itself recency-ordered, so the tail of the minFrequency bucket is the eviction candidate). Insert new key at frequency 1. Set minFrequency = 1. O(1).

TTL — Lazy + Active Expiry

Each cache entry stores a timestamp (creation or last-write time). On access, the cache checks now − timestamp > TTL — if true, the entry is a miss even if the key exists (lazy expiry). Most caches also run a background sweep (active expiry) periodically to reclaim memory from expired entries without waiting for access. The TTL duration is configured per-key or globally. Sliding TTL (reset on access) vs. absolute TTL (never reset) are two variants — sliding is appropriate for sessions, absolute for data with fixed validity windows.

TTL + LRU Combined (Redis allkeys-lru)

When memory pressure exists, Redis uses LRU to select the eviction candidate from all keys including unexpired ones. When an entry's TTL expires, it becomes a miss regardless of LRU position. The two mechanisms are independent: LRU responds to memory pressure; TTL responds to data age. If both fire on the same key simultaneously, the TTL expiry takes effect first (the key is logically gone); the LRU bookkeeping is then irrelevant for that key.

2Q — Scan-Resistant LRU Variant

2Q maintains two queues: a small FIFO probationary queue for new entries (first access), and a larger LRU protected queue for entries accessed more than once. New entries enter the probationary queue. If accessed again before eviction, they graduate to the protected LRU queue. If evicted from the probationary queue without a second access (typical for scan entries), they never pollute the protected queue. This dramatically reduces cache pollution from sequential scans without the full complexity of LFU.

11 ⚖️ Trade-offs

Every design decision has a cost. What are you giving up?
✅ Benefit❌ Cost / Limitation
LRU: Simple O(1) implementation; works well for most recency-biased workloads Vulnerable to cache pollution from sequential scans; one full table scan can evict the entire hot dataset
LFU: Higher hit rate for Zipfian (frequency-skewed) workloads; scan-resistant by design More complex O(1) implementation; frequency counters can be "stale" — old-popular entries block eviction of newer popular ones without decay
TTL: Guarantees data freshness; prevents serving stale data past a known validity window Does not respond to memory pressure; short TTL → high miss rate and cache stampede risk; long TTL → stale data risk
Approximate LRU (Redis): Low overhead; scales to millions of keys without per-key linked list traversal Not exact; under adversarial or highly skewed workloads, approximation error may reduce hit rate vs. exact LRU
LFU with decay (Redis): Adapts to changing access patterns over time; avoids stale-popularity problem Decay rate tuning is non-trivial: too fast → behaves like LRU; too slow → long tail of stale-popular entries occupies cache permanently

12 🎯 Interview Application

How do you use this concept in a design interview? What triggers it?

When an interviewer asks / says:

  • "How does your cache decide what to evict when it's full?"
  • "Your cache is only N GB — how do you make sure the hottest data stays cached?"
  • "How do you prevent stale data from being served?"
  • "You're designing a distributed cache for a social media feed — what's your eviction strategy?"

What you say / do:

In the cache design or deep dive section, proactively state your eviction policy choice and justify it with the access pattern. For social/media systems with Zipfian distributions, call out LFU. For session/auth caches with known expiry windows, call out TTL. For general-purpose caches, default to LRU but note the scan pollution risk if any batch processing touches the same data path.

"If we choose LRU, we get a simple O(1) implementation that handles recency-biased workloads well, but we risk cache pollution if our pipeline includes batch or scan jobs. For this system — a read-heavy social feed — LFU is the right call because the top 1% of content accounts for ~80% of reads, and LFU will protect those hot entries even under scan traffic."

13 ⚠️ Common Misconceptions & Gotchas

What do candidates get wrong? What nuance is the interviewer probing for?
❌
Misconception: LRU is always good enough — it's what everyone uses in production.
✅
Reality: LRU is the most common default but fails badly for frequency-skewed workloads. Redis itself recommends LFU for CDN key caching, heavily Zipfian content systems, and any workload where a small number of keys account for the majority of accesses. The right policy depends on the access distribution.
❌
Misconception: TTL is an eviction policy like LRU or LFU — pick one of the three.
✅
Reality: TTL is a freshness/consistency mechanism, not a memory management algorithm. It answers "should this entry be considered valid?", not "which entry to remove when full?" Most production caches combine TTL (for freshness) with LRU or LFU (for memory pressure) — they serve orthogonal purposes and are used together, not as alternatives.
❌
Misconception: LFU permanently retains formerly popular items because they have high counts.
✅
Reality: Without a decay mechanism, yes — this is the "historical frequency pollution" problem. But Redis LFU uses a logarithmic counter with configurable decay (lfu-decay-time) that gradually reduces counter values for entries that haven't been accessed recently, allowing stale-but-formerly-popular entries to eventually become eligible for eviction.
❌
Misconception: Eviction only matters when the cache is completely full.
✅
Reality: Eviction policy affects hit rate continuously, not just at capacity. A well-chosen policy maintains a higher hit rate at the same cache size, equivalent to getting more throughput headroom from the same hardware budget. The policy determines the steady-state composition of the cache, not just what happens during overflow.
