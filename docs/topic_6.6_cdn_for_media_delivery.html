6.6 CDN for Media Delivery
Images & Video

§0 🗺️ Topic Overview

Media delivery is where CDNs stop being a "nice latency optimization" and become the load-bearing wall of the architecture. Images and video dominate the byte volume of most consumer systems — a single 1080p stream is ~5 Mbps, and a viral image can be requested billions of times. The challenge is twofold: images must be transformed into many variants and cached at high hit rates; video must be encoded into a ladder of bitrates, chunked into segments, and streamed adaptively so playback survives changing network conditions. Master this by understanding HLS/DASH adaptive bitrate streaming, edge image transformation, and origin shielding for large objects.

  • 1. Why media is different from static assets — A CSS file is a few KB and identical for everyone; a video is gigabytes with dozens of renditions consumed as a stream. Delivery model, cache strategy, and origin protection all differ.
  • 2. Adaptive Bitrate Streaming (HLS & DASH) — The key mechanism. Video is split into 2–10s segments, each encoded at multiple bitrates; the player fetches a manifest and switches renditions per-segment based on measured bandwidth. Every segment is a cacheable HTTP object.
  • 3. Image transformation & on-the-fly variants — Resize, crop, and reformat (WebP/AVIF) at the edge on first request, then cache keyed by transform params. "Transform once, serve many" — and constrain variants to protect hit rate.
  • 4. Origin shielding & cache hierarchy — Media misses are expensive. A shield tier consolidates misses so origin sees one fetch per object, not one per PoP. This is multi-level caching (5.8) applied to media.
  • 5. VOD vs. live, cost & DRM — VOD is fully cacheable; live needs real-time segmenting and low-latency variants. Signed URLs / token auth protect paid content at the edge without killing cacheability.

§5 🧠 Core Definition

CDN media delivery is the practice of distributing images and video to globally dispersed users by caching them at edge locations — where images are transformed into device-appropriate variants at the edge, and video is transcoded into a ladder of bitrates and split into short, individually cacheable HTTP segments that a client streams adaptively based on its current network conditions.

🖼️ Images transform + cache

One master in object storage → edge generates per-device variants (resize, crop, WebP/AVIF) on first request, cached by transform params.

⚠️ Variant explosion kills hit rate — constrain to named presets.

🎬 Video ABR segments

Transcoded into a bitrate ladder, split into 2–10s segments listed by a manifest; player switches quality per segment.

✅ Every segment is an immutable HTTP object → caches like any static file.

§6 📦 Core Concepts

Adaptive Bitrate Streaming (ABR)

The client, not the server, decides quality. The video is pre-encoded at multiple bitrates ("the ladder"), each split into aligned short segments. The player continuously measures how fast segments arrive and picks the highest rendition it can sustain, switching at segment boundaries. This is why a stream downgrades to 480p on a train and jumps back to 1080p on Wi-Fi without stopping. HLS (Apple) and MPEG-DASH are the two dominant ABR protocols; both deliver over plain HTTP.

Segmentation and the Manifest

A stream is delivered as (1) a manifest/playlist — HLS .m3u8 or DASH .mpd — describing available renditions and listing segment URLs, and (2) the segments themselves (2–10s each, .ts or fragmented-MP4). The manifest is small and may change (especially for live); segments are immutable once produced. This separation is the heart of why video is CDN-friendly: each segment is just a static file the edge can cache.

📊 Bitrate Ladder × Time-Aligned Segments

Renditions are time-aligned, so the player can switch quality at any segment boundary seamlessly

Image Transformation Pipeline

Rather than storing every crop/size/format up front, image CDNs store one high-quality master and generate variants on demand. A request like image.jpg?w=400&format=webp triggers an edge transform (resize + reformat), and the output is cached keyed by those parameters. Subsequent identical requests are pure cache hits. The design tension is variant explosion — allowing arbitrary widths shatters your hit rate, so production systems constrain to named presets or a small set of breakpoints.

Origin Shield / Tiered Cache

For large media, a cache miss is very expensive (multi-GB transfer). An origin shield is a designated intermediate cache tier that all edge PoPs consult before hitting origin. It collapses N edge misses for the same object into a single origin fetch, protecting the storage tier and cutting egress cost. This is multi-level caching (5.8) at the CDN scale.

VOD vs. Live

Video-on-demand is pre-processed: transcoded, segmented, and fully cacheable ahead of time, so hit rates approach 100%. Live produces segments in real time; the pipeline transcodes and segments on the fly, manifests update continuously, and latency ("glass-to-glass") becomes a first-class concern — addressed by low-latency variants (LL-HLS, LL-DASH) using shorter segments and chunked transfer.

§7 🔍 First Principles — Why Does This Exist?

Two physical realities force media into a specialized delivery model. First, bytes: media dominates traffic volume by orders of magnitude. Text and markup are kilobytes; a two-hour movie in 4K is tens of gigabytes, and popular content is requested at planetary scale. Serving that from a central origin would saturate the origin's disks and network long before CPU, and every far-away user would pay hundreds of milliseconds of round-trip latency plus congestion. Caching media at the edge is the only physically and economically viable answer.

Second, the network is variable and video is continuous. Early video delivery streamed one fixed-bitrate file; if the user's bandwidth dropped below that bitrate, playback stalled and buffered. The insight of adaptive bitrate streaming was to stop treating video as one object and instead as a sequence of small, independently-fetched segments, each available at several qualities. Now the client reacts to bandwidth in real time — dropping quality to keep playing rather than freezing. Crucially, this also made video cacheable: because segments are ordinary immutable HTTP objects, the entire CDN machinery built for static files suddenly works for video. Images have a parallel story — the explosion of device sizes and modern formats (WebP, AVIF) made pre-generating every variant impractical, so transformation moved to the edge: computed once per variant, then cached.

§8 🗺️ Mental Models

Model 1: Video as a Flipbook of Clips

Don't think of a stream as a river; think of it as a flipbook of short clips. Each page (segment) is a self-contained few-second file, and for each page you can choose a high-res or low-res version. The player flips pages one at a time, and before turning each page it glances at how fast the last few pages downloaded and picks the resolution accordingly. Because every page is just a file, the CDN caches pages exactly like it caches images. Where it breaks down: live streaming, where pages are drawn moments before you flip to them — now the flipbook is being written just ahead of your reading, and latency matters.

Model 2: Image Transforms as a Short-Order Kitchen

The origin object store is the pantry holding raw ingredients (master images). The edge is a short-order cook: the first time someone orders "400px WebP," the cook prepares it fresh (transform) and keeps a plate under a warmer (cache). Everyone who orders the identical dish afterward gets the warmed plate instantly. Where it breaks down: if every customer orders a slightly different size, the cook is always cooking from scratch and the warmer is useless — the argument for a fixed menu (named presets) instead of arbitrary transforms.

Model 3: Origin Shield as a Wholesale Buyer

Imagine 200 corner shops (edge PoPs) that each want to sell the same rare product. Without coordination, all 200 phone the distant factory (origin) — 200 shipments. With an origin shield, they all order from one regional wholesaler; the wholesaler places a single factory order and distributes to all 200 shops. The factory (your storage tier) sees one request instead of 200. Where it breaks down: the wholesaler is now a potential regional bottleneck — real CDNs use a tier of shields, not one.

§9 ⚙️ How It Works — Mechanics

🎬 HLS/DASH Playback Flow (ABR in action)

Fetch manifest → start low → measure per segment → switch up/down at the boundary → repeat

Image Request Path (transform + cache)

Client requests photo.jpg?w=400&format=auto (or the CDN infers format from the Accept header). The edge checks its cache keyed by (object, width, format, quality, DPR) — on hit, return immediately. On miss, the edge (or an origin-side transform service) fetches the master, resizes/crops/re-encodes to the requested variant, stores the result with a long TTL, and returns it. All subsequent identical requests are cache hits; new variants pay the transform cost once each.

Video VOD Ingest (offline, before anyone watches)

Source uploaded to origin → transcoder produces a bitrate ladder (240p @ ~300 Kbps up to 4K @ ~20 Mbps, plus audio tracks) → each rendition is segmented into aligned 2–10s chunks → a manifest (.m3u8/.mpd) is written listing renditions and segment URLs. Segments + manifest land in origin storage, ready to be pulled into the CDN on first request.

🛡️ Origin Shield — Collapsing Cold-Object Misses

The shield deduplicates misses (request collapsing) so the storage tier is fetched once, not per-PoP

Manifest vs. Segment TTL (the critical detail)

Segments are immutable → long/near-infinite TTL, cache aggressively. VOD manifest → moderate TTL (rarely changes). Live manifest → very short TTL or no-cache, because it updates every few seconds as new segments are produced. Getting these backwards is a classic failure: a stale manifest freezes playback; uncached segments melt the origin.

⚠️ Live latency: A live encoder/packager produces segments in real time. Low-latency modes (LL-HLS / LL-DASH) use ~1s or sub-second partial segments plus chunked transfer to cut glass-to-glass delay from ~30s down to 2–5s — at the cost of lower cache efficiency and tighter timing.

§11 ⚖️ Trade-offs

⚖️ VOD vs. Live — Key Dimensions

Green = advantage, orange = acceptable, red = cost/limitation

✅ Benefit❌ Cost / Limitation
Segmentation makes video cacheable and enables seamless quality switching Transcoding into a full bitrate ladder is CPU-expensive and multiplies storage (one source → many renditions × many segments)
Edge image transforms serve perfectly-sized assets without pre-generating every variant Variant explosion destroys hit rate if arbitrary transforms are allowed; requires disciplined preset / cache-key design
Origin shielding collapses N PoP misses into 1 origin fetch, protecting storage Adds a cache tier and a potential regional bottleneck; slightly higher latency on cold pulls (extra hop through shield)
ABR keeps playback alive on poor networks by degrading quality instead of stalling Quality oscillation and startup-latency tuning are hard; aggressive switching looks janky, conservative switching wastes bandwidth
Signed URLs / DRM secure paid content at the edge Auth/DRM adds complexity and can hurt cacheability if the cache key isn't decoupled from the token; DRM licensing is operationally heavy

  • "How would you serve 4K video worldwide without buffering?"
  • "How do you efficiently deliver images sized for both mobile and desktop?"

What You Say / Do

Bring this up during high-level design of the delivery/read path, right after placing object storage for the media. For video: "I'd transcode each upload into an adaptive bitrate ladder, segment each rendition into ~4-second chunks, and write an HLS/DASH manifest. The player streams adaptively, and because every segment is an immutable HTTP object, my CDN caches them with a long TTL — I only keep the manifest short-TTL. I'd add an origin shield so a cold title doesn't hammer storage across every PoP." For images: "I'd store one master and transform to device-specific variants at the edge, caching each variant keyed by size and format, and constrain to named presets to protect hit rate." Then proactively raise the manifest-vs-segment TTL split and origin shielding to show depth.

💬 Trade-off statement (memorize this):

"By transcoding into an ABR ladder and delivering short cacheable segments, we get resilient global playback and near-100% CDN offload for VOD — at the cost of heavy upfront transcoding compute and multiplied storage. For a VOD-heavy product that's clearly worth it; for a live product I'd accept lower cache efficiency and add low-latency HLS to keep glass-to-glass delay acceptable."

§13 ⚠️ Common Misconceptions & Gotchas

❌
Misconception: Video is streamed as one continuous file straight from the server.
✅
Reality: Modern streaming delivers many short, independent HTTP segments (2–10s each) at multiple bitrates. The client reassembles and adaptively switches quality per segment. This segmentation is exactly what lets a CDN cache video like any static file.
❌
Misconception: You should cache the manifest and segments the same way.
✅
Reality: Segments are immutable → long TTL. Manifests change (especially for live) → short or no TTL. Caching a stale live manifest freezes playback; treating segments as dynamic destroys hit rate. Different TTLs is mandatory.
❌
Misconception: Adaptive bitrate means the server picks the quality based on the user's device.
✅
Reality: The client picks. The player measures real-time throughput and buffer health and requests the rendition it can sustain, switching at segment boundaries. The server just offers the ladder.
❌
Misconception: Generate every image size and format up front and store them all.
✅
Reality: That's a combinatorial explosion (sizes × formats × crops × DPR). Production systems store one master and transform on demand at the edge, caching each variant — and constrain the allowed variants to protect hit rate.
❌
Misconception: Signed URLs make content uncacheable because every URL is unique.
✅
Reality: Only if you design the cache key badly. The CDN validates the token/signature but keys the cache on the underlying object path (not the token), so authorized requests still hit a shared cached object.

§15 🧪 Self-Check Quiz

Can you answer these without looking? If not, you haven't internalized it yet. Click to reveal the hint.
1
Why is video delivered as short segments at multiple bitrates rather than as a single file, and how does that interact with the CDN?
▼ Hint
💡 Single-bitrate files stall when bandwidth drops. Segmenting into 2–10s chunks at multiple bitrates lets the client switch quality per segment to keep playing — and because each segment is an immutable HTTP object, the CDN caches it like any static file. Revisit §6 and §9 if you hesitated.
2
You're designing an image service. A PM wants to allow arbitrary width/height query params. What's the risk, and how do you mitigate it?
▼ Hint
💡 Variant explosion: every unique size/format is a distinct cache key, so hit rate collapses and the edge is constantly transforming from scratch. Mitigate by constraining to named presets / a small set of breakpoints (and snapping requested widths to the nearest allowed value). Revisit §6 (Image Transformation) and §13.
3
In HLS/DASH, what decides which bitrate the user gets at any given moment, and when does the switch happen?
▼ Hint
💡 The client decides — not the server. The player measures per-segment throughput and buffer level, then picks the highest sustainable rendition, switching at the next segment boundary (renditions are time-aligned so switches are seamless). Revisit §6 (ABR) and §13.
4
A brand-new movie is requested from 200 edge PoPs within seconds. Without origin shielding, what happens to your storage tier, and how does shielding fix it?
▼ Hint
💡 Without a shield, all 200 PoPs miss and each pulls the multi-GB object from origin → 200× egress and load spike (a thundering herd on origin storage). A shield/tier-1 cache deduplicates: it fetches origin once (request collapsing) and fans out to all edges. Origin sees one fetch. Revisit §6 (Origin Shield) and §9.
5
You cache the manifest and the segments with the same long TTL on a live stream. What breaks, and what's the correct TTL strategy?
▼ Hint
💡 The live manifest updates every few seconds as new segments are produced; a stale cached manifest points players at old segments and freezes playback. Correct strategy: manifest = very short TTL / no-cache; segments = long TTL (they're immutable). Revisit §9 (Manifest vs. segment TTL).
